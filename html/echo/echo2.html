Drank memory-tea in Lunara
I remembered my first home.

– Edele

They served it in a bowl, not a cup — pale ceramic, matte-glazed, cradled between two open hands like a secret. The server didn’t say anything. Just bowed slightly, eyes kind, and set it on the bloomstone table before me. The tea shimmered faintly, blue at the edges with soft golden flecks rising and falling like it was breathing.

I’d heard stories, of course. That memory-tea only worked if you were ready. That it didn’t show what you wanted to see — only what was waiting to be remembered. I wasn’t sure if I believed that. But as I lifted the bowl, the heat curled up into my face like an old scent I couldn’t name. The first sip tasted like honey left in rain. And then everything shifted.

I was five. I was small. I was curled on the windowsill of a place that hadn’t existed in years, watching twilight flicker off a distant skyline I’d forgotten the shape of. The smell of citrus dust and nightwind from my childhood hallway. My mother humming. My fingers wrapped around a chipped mug I used to insist was mine. It wasn’t overwhelming. It wasn’t painful. It just… was. Present. Honest. Whole.

When I opened my eyes, the tea was cold. The garden around me had gone quiet. A single bloom had fallen onto my table — pale lavender, trembling faintly in the wind. The server returned and collected the bowl without a word, though our eyes met for a moment. He didn’t ask what I saw. He didn’t need to. I left the blossom where it landed. It didn’t belong to me. But I walked away with something else: the shape of a memory I thought I’d lost forever, tucked back into the space behind my ribs. And that night, for the first time in a long time, I dreamed in the colors of my first home.
